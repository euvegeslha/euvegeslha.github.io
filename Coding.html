Donovan's Biography

Donovan Bailey is a Jamaican, Canadian who was born in Manchester Jamaica in 1967 and immigrated to Canada at the age of 13. He played basketball before graduating at Queen Elizabeth Park High School in Oakville, Ontario. In 1991 he started to compete in 100 m races as a part time sprinter, and took the sport seriously in 1994, and had a job as stockbroker and coached by Dan Pfaff.

At the Track and Field Championships that took place in Gothenburg, Sweden, Donovan won both 100m and the 4x100m relay. Donovan also broke the indoor 50m world record as a precursor to the centennial Olympics that was held in Atlanta, during a competition in Reno, Nevada in 1996 and was timed at 5.56 seconds. At the 1996 Atlanta Olympics, repeating double and setting a world record of 9.84 seconds plus 0.7m/s wind in the 100m. He restored the image that was put upon Canadian athletes, which was tarnished in 1988's Summer Olympics in Seoul by Ben Johnson, who was caught doping.

Bailey was the only other person to have all major titles in the 100m concurrent, with Carl Lewis being the first. In 1997, Mr.Bailey won a third world title with the Canadian relay team and finished second with Maurice Greene being first, in the 100m relay. He also broke his Achilles tendon in 1998, which put an end to his career, although he has made a second attempt in the year 2000 for Olympic glory, but had to drop out due to pneumonia.In 2001 he retired and is known for being a three-time World and the 2 time Olympic champion.

In Atlanta his record of 9.84 was broken in 1999, by Maurice Greene. His time was the Commonwealth record until 2005, until Asafa Powell broke it and then his was broken in 2008 by Usain Bolt at the Beijing Olympics. In the spring of 1997 he raced against Michael Johnson in a 150m race to determine who was the fastest man, which was provoked by Michael who has been in T.V promotions calling himself the fastest man in the world, because of his 200m world record. In the end he won against him with a time of 14.99s and received $1.5 million.

He stared his own company to promote amateur athletes called DBX Sport management and also started a sports clinic in Oakville,Ontario. He has been in Canada's Hall of Fame twice: Once in 2004 and another time in 2008 as part of the 1996 Summer Olympics 4x100 relay team. In the 2008 Summer Olympics for CBC Television, he worked as a track commentator. Bailey is now a father of two children, has four brothers named: Glenroy, Wilburn, O'Neill and Michael.
